About

I'm a researcher and builder working at the intersection of search, language models, and autonomous agents. I believe the most important problem in AI right now is not generating text but grounding it in evidence, and my work focuses on making that happen at web scale.

The narrative

I started my career at Microsoft where I co-created MS MARCO, a dataset and benchmark that became the standard for evaluating neural information retrieval systems. More than 10,000 researchers have used it. The core insight was simple: if you want machines to understand human information needs, you need human-generated data that captures real comprehension, not just pattern matching. This work, and the TREC Deep Learning Track I co-organized for five years, helped establish the evaluation methodology that the field still uses.

I then became obsessed with making these systems efficient enough to deploy at web scale. During my PhD at UIUC and consulting work with Neural Magic, Walmart, and Qualtrics, I developed compression methods (pruning, quantization, knowledge distillation) that achieved 4–40x inference speedups without accuracy loss. The Optimal BERT Surgeon showed you could remove most of a model's parameters using second-order information and lose almost nothing. I also explored curriculum learning for language models back in 2019–2020, before it became a standard technique in LLM training pipelines.

At Snowflake, I worked on retrieval, extraction, and generation for Cortex Search. Our team shipped Arctic-Embed, a family of open-source embedding models that matched the best proprietary alternatives—trained on just two H100 nodes over a few weeks. I pushed to release everything under Apache 2.0 with full research discussion of how we built them; the models have since reached millions of monthly downloads. We also built preference-based optimization loops using user feedback as proxy reward signals to continuously improve search quality.

Now at Zipf AI, I'm building what I think comes next: deploying agents to search and explore both internal and external data sources continuously, not just in response to queries. The system includes a closed-loop self-healing pipeline that optimizes its own search queries and crawl targets using information-gain scoring as a reward signal—essentially reinforcement learning for search agents.

What I think matters

Language models are extraordinary tools for reasoning over evidence, but they're currently deployed in a way that underserves most people. You ask a question, get an answer, and then forget. The real opportunity is persistent intelligence: systems that accumulate understanding over time, learn what matters to you specifically, and proactively surface information before you know to ask for it.

This requires advances across the stack: better evaluation (the TREC RAG Track I co-organize is working on this), more efficient inference (so persistent monitoring is economically viable), preference-aligned models (that optimize for genuine information gain rather than plausible-sounding text), and adaptive systems (that improve themselves through experience).

I believe we're moving from a world of underexpressive humans (keyword queries, 10 blue links) to overexpressive models, and the challenge is building the evaluation methodology, training pipelines, and system architectures to make that transition trustworthy.
